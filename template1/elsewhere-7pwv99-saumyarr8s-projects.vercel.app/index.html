His father, Yerrappa, taught him everything. Flow to tanks reduced. As tanks began to dry, farming became difficult. Those who could afford them dug borewells. District Commissioner ordered closure of sluices, stocking of water in tanks. This rendered Neeruganti useless to the community. Only those with borewells continued to cultivate. Those without left lands fallow.Under Neeruganti care, lake habitat flourished. Sustained biodiversity that fed the local community. Farmers relied on water for irrigation and silt as manure. Shepherds used grass for grazing. Fishermen caught native fish from waters. Now many farmlands lie empty. Those dependent on harvests now depend on rations from Public Distribution System. Many young people from Neeruganti families have migrated to cities.If the fish Poojappa was found in the lake, it was considered equivalent to Goddess Lakshmi being present. Ecosystems which flourished for centuries deteriorated in decades. It's almost impossible to find native species these days. If there is Neeruganti, everyone benefits. Panchayat appoints Neeruganti, entrusting work to him. They value his knowledge. In return he provides information to the panchayat. Conveys if there is leakage in the tank and if repairs are required for channels. The Neeruganti ensured equality in distributing water. Each farmer would get adequate water, the tank would be taken care of, the livestock would benefit, and the panchayat would benefit from revenue. Very difficult without Neeruganti. The tank becomes useless without him. Loss has a great impact on fish in tanks. Panchayats used to get 100,000 to 200,000 rupees from auctioning fish. That money was used for repairing tank bunds, desilting, paying Neeruganti, electricity, and other needs. VENKATAPPA STILL WALKS TO DODDAMMANNA KERE.FARMER CHINNA VENKATARAVANAPPA REMEMBERS WHEN IT WORKED. This is what Venkatappa knows. How to read the tank, judge water levels, and advise on crops. Based on rainfall, he would tell farmers what to cultivate. He directed maintenance, desilting, and revegetation at bunds to reduce erosion and feeder channel upkeep.
If there is no control by the Neeruganti, there will be no water in the tank and no crop for the farmers. When I was a child, paddy was cultivated over 100 acres of the command area. There were 80 farmers then. The Neeruganti was responsible for providing water to each farmer's land. His role was to inform everyone about the amount of water in the tank and what can be grown with the available water. People would collectively agree and decide on a date for sowing. The Neeruganti made sure water remained even after harvesting for livestock and domestic purposes.In the past, water was used judicially. Farmers united about sharing. We saved water for livestock to drink. Released more to higher areas, less to lower areas depending on topography and proximity to the lake. Even if the water level was very low, we made sure all farmers still received water. Still prays to Gangamma. Still knows exactly how much water the tank should hold, which farmers need what, when to release, and how to maintain. The knowledge sits in him like water behind concrete. Held, contained, going nowhere.'Water belongs to the community,' he says. It cannot belong to one person because everyone needs water. Tanks have survived for so many years because the community has cared for them. He speaks in the present tense about tanks that stopped flowing years ago. The grammar of someone whose work continues even when the work has ended. A keeper without a tank. A manager with nothing to manage. Knowledge waiting for permission that may never come.The Neeruganti would be paid 40 munthas, about 300 kilograms of grain, for every acre of paddy cultivated. So they had an incentive to keep crops from drying, ensuring water reached equally to all plots.
